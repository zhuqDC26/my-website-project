Right now we are going to discuss the different strokes in tennis. Tennis strokes are categorized into baseline groundstrokes, net shots, and point-starting serves. The six fundamental strokes are the serve, forehand, backhand, forehand volley, backhand volley, and overhead smash. Lets discuss the steps to hit a perfect shot for these strokes!

SERVES

1. The Grip and Stance

Use a Continental Grip (as if you are holding a hammer). Stand sideways to the baseline with your feet shoulder-width apart and your front foot angled at about 45 degrees. Your weight should be predominantly on your back foot as you prepare.

2. The Ball Toss

Hold the ball in your fingers (not the palm) and use a straight-arm motion originating from your shoulder to lift it up. Toss the ball to around the "1 o'clock" position in the clock face above your head, placing it just in front of your body.

3. The "Trophy" Position

As the ball rises, coil your body sideways and bend your knees. Your hitting arm should be cocked back with the elbow aligned with your shoulders, resembling the posture of someone preparing to throw a ball.

4. The Swing and Contact

Drop the racket head down your back while simultaneously bringing your elbow forward. Explode upward with your legs and snap your arm up to hit the ball at the highest comfortable extension. Pronate your wrist (rotate your forearm and wrist) right before contact to generate power and spin.

5. The Follow-Through

Finish the stroke by bringing your racket down across your non-dominant side and landing forward into the court in an athletic ready position

FOREHANDS

1. Unit Turn

Pivot your shoulders and hips sideways while turning your torso to store energy in your core.

2. Set Position

Bring the racket back in preparation, keeping your non-dominant hand pointing forward for balance and spacing.

3. Racket Drop

Let your racket drop into a "slot" position below the height of the ball to prepare for the upward swing.

4. Racket Flip

Uncoil your hips and flip the racket head forward and upward to generate heavy acceleration.

5. Contact Point

Hit the ball out in front of your body at hip height with your wrist firm and strings facing the target.

6. Arm Extension

Drive your hitting arm forward and upward along the ball's path to ensure depth and topspin.

7. Modern Finish

Allow your racket to wrap naturally around your opposite shoulder, keeping a relaxed follow-through.

BACKHANDS

1. The Grip

For a right-handed player, place your left hand at the top of the racket (using a handshake grip) and your right hand at the bottom. Your non-dominant hand provides the power and control.

2. The Unit Turn

As the ball approaches, pivot your torso and shoulders sideways so your chest faces the side fence. This coils your core to store energy.

3. The Swing

Drop your hands and allow the racket to dip below the level of the ball. As you swing forward, uncoil your hips and shoulders.

4. Contact Point

Strike the ball slightly in front of your body and to your side. Your left arm (as a righty) should drive the swing, and your weight should transfer from your back foot to your front foot.

5. Follow Through

Finish the swing with both arms extending forward before bending, allowing the racket to come around and end high near your right shoulder

FOREHAND/BACKHAND VOLLEYS

1. Continental Grip

: Hold the racket like a hammer. The base knuckle of your index finger should rest on the second bevel of the handle. This allows you to smoothly transition between forehands, backhands, and overheads without changing your hand placement mid-point.

2. Ready Position

: Keep your feet wider than shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent, and your weight on the balls of your feet. Hold the racket out in front of your chest with both hands. Your elbows should remain comfortably out in front of your body line, never tucked into your ribs

3. Prepartion and Turn

Turn your shoulders slightly to the right (about 45 degrees) as you see the ball coming. Do not bring the racket back past your shoulder. The most common mistake is taking a huge swing. Your elbow must stay ahead of your body to maintain stability and prevent hitting the ball late. Lock your wrist back so the racket face forms a solid angle with your forearm. Set the racket head up at roughly a 45-degree angle

4. Footwork and Contact

React to the ball by stepping out with your right foot first (the outside leg) to establish balance. Then, step forward and across with your left foot as you make contact. Strike the ball slightly out in front and to your right side. Do not push or punch wildly through the ball. Instead, think of it as "catching" the ball on your strings. Squeeze your fingers tight on the handle at the moment of impact and "freeze" your racket movement to control the redirection. Keep the racket face slightly open. Move the racket on a slight high-to-low path to slice under the ball, keeping your shot deep and low over the net.

TO BE HONEST THE ONLY DIFFERENCE OF FOREHAND AND BACKHAND VOLLEYS IS WITH FOREHAND VOLLEYS, YOU HIT ON YOUR DOMINANT SIDE AND WITH BACKHAND VOLLEYS, YOU HIT WITH YOUR NON DOINANT SIDE.

OVERHEAD SMASH

1. Set the Grip and Turn

Grip: Use a Continental Grip (the "hammer" grip), which is the exact same grip used for serves. This eliminates the need to waste time switching grips.

Turn: As soon as you recognize a lob, pivot your feet and turn sideways so your non-dominant shoulder points toward the net.

2. Prepare and Track the Ball

Extend your non-hitting arm up to point directly at the incoming ball. This helps track the ball and keeps your head up. Take the racket back to your ear in a "trophy pose," with your racket head pointed upward. Avoid letting your racket drop too early, as this costs you power.

3. Get in Position

Use side steps to make small adjustments, or cross-over steps (similar to a quarterback dropping back) if you need to retreat quickly. Always try to position your body slightly behind and underneath the ball.

4. Swing and Contact

Strike the ball in front of your body with yourp> dominant arm fully extended at the highest possible point. Snap your wrist forward (pronate your forearm) as you make contact to generate power and control. Allow the racket to follow through naturally, ending across your opposite hip.

THE SCORING IN TENNIS

Now that we are done talking about how to hit the 6 fundamental strokes in tennis, we will now talk abou the scoring in tennis

Tennis scoring tracks a hierarchy of Points, Games, and Sets. To win a match, a player must win a required number of sets. Within each set, they must win games, and within each game, they must win points. A player must always win by a margin of at least two points or two games.

A single game is won by the first player to score four points, as long as they are ahead by at least two points. Points are called uniquely as follows:

0 points = Love

1 point = 15

2 points = 30

3 points = 40

4 points = Game (win)

Winning the first point after deuce gives a player the Advantage (called Ad-In if the server has it, or Ad-Out if the receiver has it).If the player with the advantage wins the next point, they win the game.If the opponent wins the point, the score returns to Deuce

THE MATCHES IN TENNIS

Tennis matches are categorized by their player format (Singles or Doubles) and duration (Best of 3 or Best of 5 sets). Singles features one player against another, while doubles matches feature two-player teams competing on a wider court layout.Matches are structured into sets and games, where a set is typically won by the first player or team to reach six games with a two-game lead.

1. Match Formats by Player

Singles: One player against another. The court used is narrower, as the "doubles alleys" on the sides are out of bounds.

Doubles: Two teams of two players each. The full width of the court, including the doubles alleys, is in bounds.

Mixed Doubles: A combination of male and female players on each team, played on the same court dimensions as doubles.

THE DURATION AND SETS OF TENNIS MATCHES

Tennis matches can vary in duration based on their format and the skill levels of the players. Here are the main factors that influence match duration:

1. Match Formats

Best of 3 Sets: The first player or team to win two sets wins the match. This format is common in women's matches and early rounds of men's tournaments.

Best of 5 Sets: The first player or team to win three sets wins the match. This format is typically used in men's Grand Slam matches.

2. Game Length

Games can vary in length, with some lasting only a few minutes and others extending to 10 minutes or more, especially if they go to deuce multiple times.

3. Player Styles

Players with aggressive styles may finish points quickly, while baseline players who engage in long rallies can prolong matches.
